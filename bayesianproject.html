Bayesian Whiff % Analysis: Cutting Through Small Sample Noise

Every April, baseball fans and analysts are tempted to make sweeping claims about pitchers and hitters based on very small samples. But how much of that early-season performance reflects true skill, and how much is just randomness?

Motivation

Traditional stats like strikeout rates can be highly volatile early in the season. For example, if a pitcher has faced only 30 batters, their strikeout percentage could swing wildly based on just a handful of outcomes. To address this, we use a Bayesian Hierarchical Model to incorporate prior knowledge (league averages) and update our beliefs with observed data.

Method

We modeled whiff rate (swing-and-miss percentage) as a binomial process, with each swing resulting in either a whiff or not. A Beta distribution was chosen as the prior, centered on the league-average whiff rate from 2022–2024. As new data is observed, the posterior distribution updates to reflect the pitcher’s likely true talent.

Case Study: Anthony Veneziano

Using Statcast data, we collected Veneziano’s swings and whiffs from the first week of the 2025 season and combined it with the prior distribution. The posterior distribution shrinks extreme results back toward the league average, providing a more stable estimate of his true skill.

Results

  • Prior mean (league average): ~25%
  • Observed whiff rate: sample-specific
  • Posterior mean: updated estimate reflecting both prior and observed data
  • 95% credible interval: quantifies uncertainty in true whiff rate

Takeaways

By applying Bayesian inference, we reduce the risk of overreacting to small-sample fluctuations. Instead, we generate stable, probabilistic estimates of player performance—useful for projection systems, team decision-making, and public analysis.
